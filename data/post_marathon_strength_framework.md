# Post-Marathon Strength Training Framework
## Neil Stagner - Comprehensive Recovery and Rebuild Strategy

**Document Purpose:** This framework provides enduring principles for strength training during the post-marathon recovery and rebuilding period. Use this document as a reference guide for any marathon recovery cycle, not just the current one.

**Last Updated:** November 13, 2025

---

## Core Philosophy: Marathon Recovery as Strength Opportunity

The post-marathon period is your single best opportunity to rebuild strength capacity without compromising running performance. This period allows you to:

1. Address strength deficits accumulated during race-specific training
2. Build muscular resilience for the next training cycle
3. Correct movement imbalances revealed by high-volume training
4. Develop a stronger foundation for improved running economy

**Key Principle:** While running volume and intensity drop dramatically post-race, strength training can gradually increase—creating an inverse relationship that optimizes total training stress.

## Post-Marathon Timeline: Strength Training Progression

### Phase 1: Complete Rest (Days 1-7 Post-Race)

**Strength Training Volume:** NONE

**Rationale:**
- Marathon creates significant musculoskeletal micro-damage
- Eccentric muscle damage from 26.2 miles of impact requires complete healing
- Systemic inflammation and fatigue need time to resolve
- Attempting strength work too early delays recovery and increases injury risk

**What to Do Instead:**
- Prioritize sleep, nutrition, hydration
- Light walking only (as feels comfortable)
- Gentle mobility work if desired (5-10 minutes, zero discomfort)
- Let the body heal completely

**Key Indicators You're Ready to Progress:**
- No residual muscle soreness from race
- Normal walking gait (no limping or compensation)
- RHR returned to normal baseline (within 2-3 bpm of typical)
- Sleep quality normalized
- Energy levels improving

---

### Phase 2: Movement Reintroduction (Days 8-14 Post-Race)

**Strength Training Volume:** 1-2 sessions, 20-30 minutes each, VERY light

**Training Focus:**
- Bodyweight movements only
- Movement quality and control
- Re-establishing neuromuscular patterns
- Core stability fundamentals
- Single-leg balance and coordination

**Session Structure:**
- Warm-up: 5-7 minutes dynamic mobility
- Main work: 3-4 exercises, 2 sets each, 8-12 reps
- Cool-down: 5 minutes light stretching

**Exercise Selection:**
- Glute bridges (bodyweight, controlled tempo)
- Dead bugs (core stability)
- Clamshells (glute medius activation)
- Bird dogs (core + shoulder stability)
- Single-leg balance work (30-45 sec holds)
- Wall sits (isometric quad engagement)
- Light calf raises (bodyweight, both straight and bent knee)

**Critical Rules:**
- ZERO soreness goal - if sore next day, you did too much
- RPE never exceeds 5 out of 10
- Stop if any joint discomfort appears
- Movements should feel restorative, not challenging

**Scheduling:**
- Tuesday + Friday ideal (matches your typical strength days)
- Never within 24 hours of a run (even easy runs)
- Can combine with longer mobility sessions

---

### Phase 3: Foundation Building (Weeks 3-6 Post-Race)

**Strength Training Volume:** 2 sessions per week, 40-50 minutes each

**Training Focus:**
- Rebuild baseline strength across all major patterns
- Emphasize movement quality over load
- Develop balanced strength (avoid imbalances from marathon training)
- Begin progressive loading (very conservative progression)

**Training Phase Classification:** Phase 1 - Base/Foundation

**Movement Patterns to Include:**
1. Hip hinge (RDLs, good mornings)
2. Squat variations (goblet, split squats)
3. Lunge patterns (forward, reverse, lateral)
4. Single-leg work (Bulgarian split squats, single-leg RDLs)
5. Core stability (anti-rotation, anti-extension, anti-lateral flexion)
6. Calf strengthening (both gastrocnemius and soleus)
7. Upper body push/pull (maintains balance, supports posture)

**Load Progression Strategy:**
- Week 3: Bodyweight + light resistance bands
- Week 4: Bodyweight + dumbbells (5-15 lbs)
- Week 5: Moderate dumbbells (10-25 lbs) for larger movements
- Week 6: Continue building to RPE 6-7

**Session Template:**

**Tuesday (Primary Session):**
- Warm-up: 8-10 minutes (dynamic mobility, activation)
- Movement A: Hip hinge variation - 3×8-10 @ RPE 6
- Movement B: Squat or lunge variation - 3×8-10 each leg @ RPE 6
- Movement C: Single-leg stability - 3×6-8 each leg @ RPE 6
- Movement D: Core circuit - 2-3 rounds (8-12 reps each exercise)
- Cool-down: 5 minutes

**Friday (Supporting Session):**
- Warm-up: 5-7 minutes
- Movement A: Posterior chain focus - 3×10-12 @ RPE 5-6
- Movement B: Calf work - 3×12-15 (straight + bent knee) @ RPE 6
- Movement C: Core stability - 2 rounds
- Cool-down: 5 minutes

**Coordination with Running:**
- Running volume builds gradually (week 3: 50%, week 4: 70%, week 5: 85%, week 6: 100%)
- Quality running still minimal (mostly easy runs)
- Strength takes higher priority during this window
- Schedule heavy strength 48+ hours from any running workout

---

### Phase 4: Strength Development (Weeks 7-12 Post-Race)

**Strength Training Volume:** 2 sessions per week, 45-55 minutes each

**Training Focus:**
- Build significant strength gains while running remains moderate
- Increase loading progressively
- Maintain movement quality as loads increase
- Develop muscular resilience for upcoming training phases

**Training Phase Classification:** Phase 1-2 Hybrid (Base/Early Quality)

**Load Progression:**
- Week 7-8: RPE 7, focus on consistent technique
- Week 9-10: RPE 7-8, heavier loading for primary movements
- Week 11-12: RPE 8, peak strength emphasis before quality running increases

**Key Exercises to Progress:**
- Barbell or heavy dumbbell RDLs
- Goblet or front squat variations
- Bulgarian split squats with moderate load
- Single-leg RDLs with moderate load
- Weighted step-ups
- Nordic hamstring curls (if appropriate)
- Weighted calf raises

**Session Structure:**

**Tuesday (Heavy Day):**
- Warm-up: 10 minutes
- Primary lift: Hip hinge or squat variation - 4×6-8 @ RPE 7-8
- Secondary movement: Lunge or step-up variation - 3×8-10 each @ RPE 7
- Tertiary movement: Single-leg stability - 3×8-10 each @ RPE 6-7
- Core work: 3 rounds
- Cool-down: 5 minutes

**Friday (Moderate Day):**
- Warm-up: 8 minutes
- Primary movement: Posterior chain - 3×10-12 @ RPE 6-7
- Secondary movement: Calf complex - 3×12-15 @ RPE 7
- Single-leg work: 3×8-10 each @ RPE 6
- Core finisher: 2 rounds
- Cool-down: 5 minutes

**Critical Considerations:**
- Running quality sessions begin to reappear (threshold, tempo work)
- Coordinate strength timing carefully with quality running
- If soreness interferes with running, reduce strength volume by 30%
- Monitor posterior tibialis symptoms closely during load progression

---

### Phase 5: Transition to Next Training Block (Weeks 13+ Post-Race)

**Strength Training Volume:** 2 sessions per week, 40-50 minutes

**Training Focus:**
- Maintain strength gains achieved in Phase 4
- Shift emphasis to support running-specific training
- Match strength phase to running phase (likely early quality phase)

**Programming Strategy:**
- Reduce volume slightly (sets per exercise)
- Maintain intensity (RPE 7-8 for primary movements)
- Emphasize patterns that support running economy
- Coordinate closely with quality running schedule

## Movement Pattern Library: Post-Marathon Priorities

### Posterior Chain Development (Highest Priority)

**Why It Matters:**
Marathon running creates significant quad dominance and can lead to posterior chain weakness. Rebuilding glute and hamstring strength is critical for injury prevention and running economy.

**Key Exercises:**
1. Romanian Deadlift (RDL)
   - Builds hamstring eccentric strength
   - Develops hip hinge pattern
   - Progression: bodyweight → dumbbell → barbell

2. Single-Leg RDL
   - Unilateral balance + posterior chain
   - Critical for running-specific stability
   - Progression: bodyweight → light DB → moderate DB

3. Glute Bridge / Hip Thrust
   - Glute maximus activation
   - Hip extension strength
   - Progression: bodyweight → single-leg → weighted

4. Good Mornings
   - Hip hinge with spinal stability demand
   - Hamstring and lower back strength
   - Progression: bodyweight → light DB → barbell

**Cues for Quality:**
- Maintain neutral spine (avoid rounding)
- Load hamstrings, not lower back
- Control eccentric (3-second lowering phase)
- Full hip extension at top of movement

---

### Hip Stability and Glute Medius (Second Priority)

**Why It Matters:**
Frontal plane stability prevents IT band syndrome, PTT flares, and knee valgus. Glute medius strength is essential for single-leg running mechanics.

**Key Exercises:**
1. Clamshells
   - Glute medius isolation
   - Progression: bodyweight → resistance band → heavy band

2. Side-Lying Hip Abduction
   - Frontal plane hip strength
   - Progression: bodyweight → ankle weight → cable

3. Lateral Band Walks
   - Dynamic glute medius work
   - Hip stability under movement
   - Progression: light band → medium → heavy band

4. Single-Leg Balance Progressions
   - Neuromuscular control
   - Proprioception
   - Progression: eyes open → eyes closed → unstable surface

**Cues for Quality:**
- Avoid hip hiking or compensation
- Maintain level pelvis
- Control throughout full range of motion
- Feel burn in glute medius (side of hip), not TFL (front of hip)

---

### Calf and Lower Leg Resilience (Third Priority)

**Why It Matters:**
Given your PTT history, calf and soleus strength is essential for preventing flare-ups. Marathon training often creates calf tightness without adequate strength development.

**Key Exercises:**
1. Straight-Leg Calf Raise (Gastrocnemius)
   - Standing calf raise, knee extended
   - Progression: bodyweight → single-leg → weighted

2. Bent-Knee Calf Raise (Soleus)
   - Seated or bent-knee position
   - Soleus-specific strengthening
   - Progression: bodyweight → moderate load

3. Eccentric Calf Lowers
   - Build eccentric strength
   - Tendon resilience
   - Progression: two-leg up, one-leg down → single-leg full ROM

4. Tibialis Anterior Work
   - Dorsiflexion strength
   - Balance anterior/posterior lower leg
   - Progression: toe raises → resistance band → weighted

**Cues for Quality:**
- Full range of motion (deep stretch at bottom, full rise at top)
- Control eccentric phase (3 seconds down)
- Avoid bouncing or momentum
- If PTT discomfort appears, stop immediately and reassess

---

### Core Stability (Integrated Throughout)

**Why It Matters:**
Core stability supports efficient running posture, reduces energy leaks, and prevents compensatory movement patterns.

**Key Exercise Categories:**

1. Anti-Extension (Prevents lumbar hyperextension)
   - Dead bugs
   - Planks (front, high, low)
   - Rollouts (progression: kneeling → standing)

2. Anti-Rotation (Prevents trunk rotation under load)
   - Pallof press
   - Half-kneeling chops
   - Single-arm farmer carries

3. Anti-Lateral Flexion (Prevents side-to-side collapse)
   - Side planks
   - Single-arm overhead carries
   - Copenhagen planks (advanced)

**Cues for Quality:**
- Brace core, breathe naturally (don't hold breath)
- Maintain neutral spine
- No compensation through shoulders or hips
- Quality over duration (perfect 20 seconds > sloppy 60 seconds)

## Recovery Monitoring: When to Adjust Strength Programming

### Indicators to Reduce Strength Volume/Intensity:

1. Resting Heart Rate Changes:
   - RHR elevated >5 bpm above baseline → reduce volume 40%, RPE to 5-6
   - RHR elevated 3-5 bpm → reduce volume 20%, avoid max efforts

2. Sleep Quality:
   - <6.5 hours or <75% efficiency → conservative strength day
   - Multiple nights of poor sleep → skip heavy session, do light activation only

3. Running-Related Fatigue:
   - Recent long run (>15 miles) → avoid heavy leg work for 48 hours
   - Recent quality session (threshold/intervals) → schedule strength 24+ hours after
   - Unusually high running volume week → reduce strength to maintenance mode

4. Muscular Soreness:
   - Moderate soreness from previous strength session → delay next session 24-48 hours
   - Severe soreness → skip strength, address with mobility and recovery
   - Soreness interfering with running gait → you overdid it, scale back significantly

5. Posterior Tibialis Warning Signs:
   - Medial ankle discomfort during or after strength work → stop immediately
   - PTT tenderness next morning → modify calf work, reduce single-leg loading
   - Persistent symptoms → consult PT, focus on controlled mobility

### Decision Framework: Conservative vs. Moderate vs. Full

When fatigue or recovery concerns arise, always provide three options:

**Conservative (Choose if 2+ warning signs present):**
- 15-20 minute activation circuit
- Bodyweight only, RPE 4-5
- Focus: movement quality, blood flow, feel-good session
- Example: glute bridges, clamshells, dead bugs, light mobility

**Moderate (Choose if 1 warning sign, or uncertain):**
- 30-35 minute session
- Reduce planned volume by 40-50%
- RPE 5-6 maximum
- Skip heaviest sets, focus on technique

**Full (Choose if well-recovered, no warning signs):**
- Execute planned session as written
- RPE 7-8 for primary movements
- Full volume and intensity

## Integration with Other Coaching Domains

### Coordination with Running Coach

**Weeks 1-2 Post-Marathon:**
- Running: Complete rest or very light easy jogging
- Strength: None (week 1), light reintroduction (week 2)
- No coordination needed - both are minimal

**Weeks 3-6 Post-Marathon:**
- Running: Building easy volume, no quality sessions
- Strength: Foundation building phase (higher emphasis)
- Coordination: Schedule strength sessions on non-running days when possible, or 4+ hours after easy runs

**Weeks 7-12 Post-Marathon:**
- Running: Introducing threshold, tempo, and early quality work
- Strength: Peak strength development window
- Coordination: Critical scheduling - heavy strength Tuesday, quality running Thursday, moderate strength Friday, long run Saturday

**Weeks 13+ Post-Marathon:**
- Running: Race-specific training begins
- Strength: Transitions to support running (reduced volume, maintained intensity)
- Coordination: Strength serves running - always defer to running schedule

### Coordination with Mobility Coach

- **Post-race weeks 1-2:** Mobility takes precedence over strength (gentle, restorative)
- **Weeks 3-6:** Mobility preps for strength sessions (include 8-10 min warm-up mobility before strength)
- **Weeks 7-12:** Mobility supports heavy loading (focus on hip mobility, thoracic mobility, calf/ankle work)
- **Ongoing:** Sunday mobility session remains your comprehensive flexibility work

### Coordination with Nutrition Coach

- **Post-race week 1:** Prioritize recovery nutrition, adequate protein (1.2-1.4 g/kg), carbs for healing
- **Weeks 2-6:** Protein intake supports muscle rebuilding (target 120-140g/day)
- **Weeks 7-12:** Fuel strength sessions appropriately (carbs 1-2 hours before, protein within 2 hours after)
- **Monitor:** Body weight trend - avoid unintended loss during strength building phase

## Long-Term Strength Progression Strategy

### Annual Cycle: Strength Emphasis Periods

Your training year should include strategic strength emphasis windows:

**Post-Marathon Recovery (6-12 weeks):**
- Highest strength training emphasis
- Build maximal strength capacity
- Create durability for next training cycle

**Early Base Building (4-6 weeks):**
- Maintain strength gains from post-marathon period
- Moderate emphasis (2 sessions/week)
- Foundation patterns, RPE 6-7

**Late Base / Early Quality (6-8 weeks):**
- Strength supports threshold and tempo development
- Maintain strength, prevent interference
- 2 sessions/week, carefully timed

**Race-Specific Training (6-10 weeks):**
- Minimal strength volume
- Maintain neuromuscular patterns
- 1-2 sessions/week, RPE 6-7

**Taper (1-3 weeks):**
- Dramatically reduced or eliminated
- Only light activation if anything

**Race Week:**
- Nothing

**Post-Race:**
- Restart cycle

### Progressive Overload Principles

Strength progression should follow these principles throughout the year:

1. **Movement Quality First:** Always establish proper mechanics before adding load
2. **Volume Before Intensity:** Add sets/reps before increasing weight
3. **Unilateral Progressions:** Master bilateral movements before advancing to single-leg variations
4. **Tempo Manipulation:** Use eccentric emphasis (3-1-1-0) to increase stimulus without adding load
5. **Conservative Jumps:** Increase load by 5-10% when you can complete all sets/reps at RPE <7

### Multi-Year Development Vision

**Year 1 (Current):** Establish consistent strength practice, build baseline capacity, prevent injury
**Year 2:** Increase strength capacity during post-marathon windows, support faster race paces
**Year 3+:** Maintain high strength levels year-round, minimal strength training maintains significant capacity

Your long-term goal: Become a runner with exceptional strength-to-weight ratio, muscular resilience, and movement quality that supports decades of healthy running.

## Special Considerations for Your Profile

### Posterior Tibialis Tendinopathy (PTT) Management

**During Post-Marathon Strength Building:**

**Green Light (Safe to Progress):**
- No symptoms during or after sessions
- Normal walking gait
- No tenderness to palpation along medial ankle/arch

**Yellow Light (Modify):**
- Mild discomfort during single-leg work
- Slight stiffness post-session
- Action: Reduce single-leg loading 50%, avoid eccentric calf work, increase PT exercises

**Red Light (Stop/Reassess):**
- Pain during strength exercises
- Compensatory movement patterns
- Next-day limping or significant discomfort
- Action: Stop strength work, return to PT protocol, consult physical therapist

**Protective Strategies:**
- Always include calf/ankle warm-up (5+ minutes)
- Avoid sudden load increases
- Progress single-leg work very conservatively
- Monitor symptoms closely during weeks 7-12 (heavy loading phase)
- If PTT flares, regress to bodyweight + resistance band work until resolved

### Newborn Sleep Variability

**Sleep-Adjusted Strength Recommendations:**

**Poor Sleep Night (<5 hours or <70% efficiency):**
- Skip planned strength session OR
- Execute conservative option (20 minutes, bodyweight, RPE 4-5)
- Reason: Strength training is catabolic stress; without adequate recovery, it's counterproductive

**Moderate Sleep (5-6.5 hours or 70-75% efficiency):**
- Execute moderate option (30-35 min, reduced volume)
- Avoid max effort sets
- Focus on technique and movement quality

**Good Sleep (>6.5 hours, >75% efficiency):**
- Execute full session as planned
- This is your window to build - take advantage when recovery supports it

**Philosophy:** Strength training is a privilege earned through adequate recovery. When sleep is compromised, protect your health and running first.

### Wrist Surgery Considerations

**Exercises to Modify:**
- Avoid heavy barbell front squats (wrist extension demand)
- Use straps for heavy RDLs if grip is limiting factor
- Prefer dumbbell variations for most movements
- Use safety squat bar if available for loaded squats
- Push-up variations: use handles or fists instead of flat palms

**Safe Alternatives:**
- Goblet squats (no wrist strain)
- Dumbbell RDLs (neutral grip)
- Trap bar deadlifts (if available)
- Machine-based pushing movements

## Key Success Metrics: Post-Marathon Strength Block

By the end of the 12-week post-marathon strength building period, you should achieve:

**Quantitative Markers:**
1. Return to baseline strength levels from pre-marathon taper (weeks 3-6)
2. Exceed pre-marathon strength by 15-20% (weeks 7-12)
3. Complete 2 strength sessions per week with 90%+ consistency
4. Zero strength-related injuries or setbacks
5. Zero PTT flare-ups attributable to strength training

**Qualitative Markers:**
1. Improved single-leg stability and balance
2. Stronger, more controlled movement patterns
3. Reduced fatigue during easy runs (improved muscular endurance)
4. Better running form under fatigue (stronger core, stable hips)
5. Confidence in strength capacity entering next training cycle

**Running Integration Markers:**
1. Strength training never compromises quality running sessions
2. No excessive soreness interfering with running schedule
3. Perceived running economy improvement (less effort at same paces)
4. Enhanced durability during long runs

## Summary: The Post-Marathon Strength Advantage

The marathon recovery period is not a training void—it's your most valuable strength-building window of the entire year. Use it wisely:

1. **Respect the recovery timeline:** Don't rush strength work in week 1-2
2. **Maximize the opportunity:** Weeks 7-12 are your peak strength building window
3. **Coordinate intelligently:** Strength training serves running, not the opposite
4. **Monitor continuously:** Use RHR, sleep, and subjective feel to guide daily decisions
5. **Build for the long term:** Every post-marathon cycle compounds your strength capacity

When executed properly, you'll emerge from the post-marathon period stronger, more resilient, and better prepared for the next training cycle than you've ever been.

The marathon breaks you down. The recovery period builds you back better.
