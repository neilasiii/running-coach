# Post-Marathon 2-Week Detailed Nutrition Plan

**Race:** Central Florida Legends Skunk Ape Marathon - November 23, 2025
**Goal:** 4:00 (A goal) or 4:30 (B goal) finish
**Expected Finish Time:** ~4 hours
**Body Weight:** 166 lbs (75 kg)
**Dietary Requirements:** Gluten-free, Dairy-free

This plan provides specific daily nutrition guidance for the 2 weeks following your marathon. It coordinates with expected training load reduction and supports optimal recovery.

---

## RACE DAY - November 23, 2025

### Pre-Race Fueling (3 hours before race start)

**Target:** 2-3 g/kg carbohydrate (150-225g), minimal fiber/fat, familiar foods only

**Option 1: Simple and Proven**
- 2 slices gluten-free bread, toasted (40g carbs)
- 2 tbsp almond butter (6g carbs, familiar fat source)
- 1 large banana (30g carbs)
- 8 oz orange juice (26g carbs)
- Water (16 oz)
- **Total: ~100g carbs**

**Option 2: Larger Pre-Race Meal**
- 1.5 cups cooked white rice (65g carbs)
- 3 oz grilled chicken breast (protein, minimal fiber)
- 1 tbsp honey drizzled on rice (17g carbs)
- 1 cup applesauce, unsweetened (28g carbs)
- Water (16 oz)
- **Total: ~110g carbs**

**Option 3: Oatmeal-Based**
- 1 cup certified gluten-free oats, cooked (54g carbs)
- 1 cup non-dairy milk, fortified (2g carbs)
- 2 tbsp maple syrup (26g carbs)
- 1 medium banana, sliced (27g carbs)
- Water (16 oz)
- **Total: ~110g carbs**

**Timing:** Finish eating 2.5-3 hours before gun time. Sip water until 30 min before start.

### 60-90 Minutes Before Start

- 1 gel (Precision Fuel or Huma) (22-25g carbs)
- Small sips of water (4-8 oz total)
- Optional: 2-4 Base Salt or FastChews for sodium preload

### During Race (4-hour marathon)

**Carbohydrate Target:** 40-60g per hour (160-240g total for 4 hours)

**Hydration Target:** 16-24 oz per hour based on sweat rate and conditions

**Gel Schedule (every 35-40 minutes):**
- Mile 4-5 (35 min): Gel #1 (25g carbs) + water
- Mile 8-9 (1:10): Gel #2 (25g carbs) + water
- Mile 13 (1:55): Gel #3 (25g carbs) + water + electrolytes
- Mile 17 (2:35): Gel #4 (25g carbs) + water
- Mile 21 (3:10): Gel #5 (25g carbs) + water + electrolytes
- Mile 24-25 (3:40): Optional Gel #6 if energy drops

**Total during-race carbs:** 125-150g (meets 40-60g/hour target)

**Electrolyte Strategy:**
- Base Salts or FastChews at miles 6, 13, 20 (300mg sodium each)
- Take with water at aid stations
- Total sodium: ~900-1200mg during race

**Aid Station Protocol:**
- Water at every station (small sips, don't chug)
- Walk through aid stations to ensure proper intake
- Practice grabbing cups during final taper runs

### Immediate Post-Finish (Within 30 Minutes)

**Priority:** 1.0-1.2 g/kg carbohydrate (75-90g) + 20-30g protein

**Post-Finish Recovery Pack (prepare in advance, give to support crew):**
- 2 gels or 16 oz sports drink (50g carbs)
- 1 large banana (30g carbs)
- 16 oz chocolate non-dairy milk OR protein shake (25g protein, 30g carbs)
- Salty snack (pretzels, crackers, chips) - sodium and palatability
- **Total: ~110g carbs, 25g protein**

**If stomach allows immediately:**
Consume above within 30 minutes. If nauseous, prioritize liquid calories (sports drink + protein shake), then solid food as tolerated.

### Post-Race Hours 2-6

**Target:** Additional 1.0-1.5 g/kg carbs (75-110g), 20-30g protein, aggressive rehydration

**Meal Example (2-3 hours post-race):**
- 6 oz grilled salmon (40g protein) - omega-3 anti-inflammatory bonus
- 2 cups white rice (90g carbs)
- 1 cup steamed broccoli
- 1 tbsp olive oil
- Sea salt to taste
- 16 oz water or herbal tea
- **Total: ~90g carbs, 40g protein**

**Hydration Goal (Hours 0-6):**
- Drink 150% of estimated weight loss
- Estimate: 4-5 lbs lost = 64-80 oz total fluid over 6 hours
- Include sodium with fluids (electrolyte drinks, salted foods, bone broth)
- Monitor urine color: should be pale yellow by bedtime

### Evening of Race Day

**Light snack before bed (if hungry):**
- 1 cup tart cherry juice (sleep aid, anti-inflammatory)
- Small bowl of white rice with honey or fruit
- Handful of cashews

**Total Race Day Nutrition Summary:**
- Carbohydrate: 600-700g (8-9 g/kg - very high, appropriate for race day)
- Protein: 120-140g (1.6-1.8 g/kg)
- Hydration: 120-150 oz total fluid
- Sodium: 2000-3000mg

## Hydration and Electrolyte Protocol

### Daily Hydration Targets

**General guideline:** Body weight in lbs ÷ 2 = oz per day minimum
- For 166 lbs: ~85 oz daily baseline
- Add 16-24 oz for each 30 min of activity
- Increase in warm/humid conditions

### Hydration Schedule

- Upon waking: 16 oz water
- Mid-morning: 16 oz
- With lunch: 16 oz
- Afternoon: 16 oz
- With dinner: 16 oz
- Evening: 8-16 oz (adjust to avoid sleep disruption)

### Electrolyte Considerations

**Sodium needs:** 2000-3000mg daily during recovery week, then 1500-2500mg ongoing

**Sources:**
- Salted foods (natural sea salt on meals)
- Bone broth (1 cup = 300-500mg)
- Electrolyte drinks (use around runs)
- Base Salts or FastChews if feeling depleted

**Signs of adequate hydration:**
- Pale yellow urine
- Regular urination (6-8 times daily)
- No excessive thirst
- Good energy levels
- Normal skin elasticity

**Signs of dehydration:**
- Dark yellow urine
- Headaches
- Fatigue
- Dizziness
- Rapid heart rate

## Supplements and Recovery Aids

### Recommended for Post-Marathon Recovery

**Tart Cherry Juice:**
- Dose: 8-12 oz daily, first 7-10 days
- Timing: Evening (supports sleep and inflammation)
- Benefit: Reduces inflammation, improves sleep quality

**Omega-3 (EPA/DHA):**
- Dose: 2-3g combined EPA/DHA daily
- Source: Fatty fish 2-3x/week OR algae-based supplement (DF option)
- Benefit: Anti-inflammatory, supports cellular repair

**Vitamin D:**
- Continue any existing supplementation (3000-5000 IU daily if deficient)
- Important for bone health, immune function, recovery

**Probiotic:**
- Consider if experienced GI issues during race
- Supports gut barrier recovery
- Choose DF option

### Optional/Situational

**Protein Powder:**
- Use for convenience, not necessity
- Choose high-quality GF/DF blend (pea + rice + hemp)
- Useful for post-run recovery when whole food meal delayed

**Creatine:**
- If already using, continue at 5g daily
- If not currently using, wait until resuming structured training

**Collagen:**
- Emerging evidence for tendon/ligament support
- 10-15g daily if interested (check source for DF)

### NOT Recommended During Recovery

**High-dose antioxidants (Vitamin C, E megadoses):**
- May interfere with training adaptation
- Get antioxidants from whole food sources instead

**Excessive NSAIDs (ibuprofen, etc.):**
- Can impair muscle repair and adaptation
- Use sparingly, only if truly needed for pain management

## Monitoring Recovery Progress

### Key Metrics to Track

**Daily (first week):**
- Resting heart rate (upon waking)
- Body weight (morning, after bathroom, before eating)
- Subjective energy level (1-10 scale)
- Soreness level (1-10 scale)
- Sleep quality (hours and perceived restfulness)

**Weekly:**
- Weight trend (should stabilize within 2-3 lbs of pre-race)
- Average RHR (should trend toward baseline)
- Energy and mood (should improve week over week)

### Expected Recovery Timeline

**Days 1-3:**
- Significant muscle soreness (peak day 2-3)
- Elevated RHR (5-10 bpm above baseline)
- Fatigue and low motivation normal
- Increased hunger or decreased appetite both possible

**Days 4-7:**
- Soreness beginning to resolve
- RHR trending downward
- Energy improving
- Sleep normalizing (if newborn cooperates!)

**Days 8-14:**
- Minimal soreness remaining
- RHR near baseline (within 2-3 bpm)
- Energy levels good
- Ready for light running

### Red Flags Requiring Adjustment

**Nutritional red flags:**
- Weight loss exceeding 3 lbs in first week
- Persistent low energy beyond day 7
- Ongoing appetite suppression
- Signs of inadequate fueling (see below)

**Signs of Low Energy Availability:**
- Irritability, mood changes
- Persistent fatigue
- Poor sleep quality
- Decreased libido
- Irregular menstrual cycle (if applicable)
- Prolonged elevated RHR
- Increased susceptibility to illness

**If red flags appear:**
- Increase total calorie intake by 200-300 daily
- Ensure eating at least 1.6 g/kg protein
- Don't restrict any macronutrient groups
- Consider consultation with sports dietitian
- Delay return to structured training until resolved

## Final Notes and Adjustments

### Individual Variability

This plan provides structure, but individual needs vary based on:
- Actual race effort and finish time
- Environmental conditions on race day
- Recovery rate (influenced by sleep, stress, genetics)
- Training history and adaptation
- Body composition goals

**Adjust based on your response:** If constantly hungry, increase portions slightly. If uncomfortably full, reduce portions. The goal is adequacy, not rigid macro hitting.

### Work-Life Integration

**Workday nutrition (Mon-Thu, 0700-1730):**
- Pack lunch and snacks the night before
- Use meal prep to simplify workday eating
- Keep emergency snacks at office (nuts, bars, fruit)
- Hydration: keep water bottle at desk

**Newborn considerations:**
- Accept imperfect nutrition when sleep-deprived
- Keep easy options readily available
- Don't skip meals even when exhausted
- Partner/family support crucial during this period

### Post-Recovery Transition

**Week 3-4 (Dec 8-21):**
- Begin gradual increase in training volume
- Adjust carbohydrate intake upward as mileage increases
- Return to periodized nutrition approach (match intake to training days)
- Consider reassessing VDOT and energy needs

**Return to structured training (Week 4+):**
- Reference endurance nutrition coach for training-phase nutrition
- Lessons learned from this recovery inform next training cycle
- Document what worked well for future marathons

## Questions to Assess Recovery Progress

**End of Week 1 (Nov 30):**
- Is your RHR trending back toward baseline?
- Has muscle soreness significantly improved?
- Are you sleeping reasonably well?
- Is your energy level improving?
- Is your weight stable?

**End of Week 2 (Dec 7):**
- Do easy runs feel comfortable and controlled?
- Is your appetite normalized?
- Has RHR returned to within 2-3 bpm of baseline?
- Do you feel mentally ready to resume training?
- Any lingering injury concerns?

If the answer to these questions is mostly "yes," you're recovering well and nutrition is supporting the process. If mostly "no," consider extending recovery period and increasing energy intake.
