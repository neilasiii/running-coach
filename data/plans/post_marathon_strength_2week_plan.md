# Post-Marathon Strength Training: Detailed 2-Week Plan
## Neil Stagner - Days 1-14 After Marathon (Nov 24 - Dec 7, 2025)

**Marathon Date:** November 23, 2025 (Central Florida Legends Skunk Ape Marathon)
**Plan Start Date:** November 24, 2025 (Day 1 Post-Race)
**Plan End Date:** December 7, 2025 (Day 14 Post-Race)

**Document Purpose:** This plan provides specific daily guidance for the critical first two weeks after your marathon. It transitions you from complete rest to gentle strength reintroduction while prioritizing recovery.

---

## Week 1 (Days 1-7): Complete Rest from Strength Training

### Overarching Philosophy - Week 1

**NO STRENGTH TRAINING THIS WEEK.**

Your body has just endured 26.2 miles of continuous eccentric loading. Every muscle fiber, tendon, and connective tissue structure needs complete healing time. Attempting strength work during this window will:
- Delay muscular recovery
- Increase injury risk
- Compromise your ability to rebuild effectively in weeks 2-6
- Create accumulated fatigue that extends total recovery time

**What You SHOULD Do This Week:**
1. Prioritize sleep (aim for 7+ hours when possible, even with newborn care)
2. Maintain excellent nutrition (protein: 120-140g/day, adequate carbs, hydration)
3. Light walking as feels comfortable (no pain, no limping)
4. Very gentle mobility work if desired (5-10 minutes, zero discomfort)
5. Celebrate your marathon achievement

**What to Monitor:**
- Resting heart rate returning to baseline (should drop toward 45-46 bpm by day 5-7)
- Muscle soreness resolution (quads, glutes, calves should improve daily)
- Normal walking gait restoration (no compensatory patterns)
- Energy levels (expect fatigue days 1-4, improvement days 5-7)
- Sleep quality (may be disrupted by race recovery + newborn care)

## Week 2 (Days 8-14): Gentle Strength Reintroduction

### Overarching Philosophy - Week 2

This week marks the transition from complete rest to gentle movement reintroduction. The goals are:

1. Re-establish neuromuscular patterns (your body remembers how to move, just needs gentle reminder)
2. Promote blood flow to muscles without creating soreness
3. Begin activation of key running support muscles (glutes, core, calves)
4. Build confidence for progressive loading in weeks 3-6

**Critical Rules for Week 2:**
- ZERO soreness goal (if you're sore the next day, you did too much)
- Bodyweight only (no external resistance except light bands)
- RPE never exceeds 5 out of 10 (should feel easy, almost too easy)
- Stop immediately if any joint discomfort appears
- Sessions are 20-30 minutes maximum
- Movements should feel restorative, not challenging

**Strength Session Frequency:** 2 sessions (Tuesday Day 10, Friday Day 13)

### Day 10 (Tuesday, December 3) - Race Day +10

**FIRST STRENGTH SESSION**

**Duration:** 20-30 minutes
**Intensity:** RPE 4-5 (easy, controlled)
**Load:** Bodyweight only
**Goal:** Movement reintroduction, activation, confidence building

---

**SESSION 1: Movement Reintroduction**

**Pre-Session Check:**
- [ ] Slept reasonably well (5+ hours)
- [ ] No residual soreness from any recent activity
- [ ] RHR at baseline (45-47 bpm)
- [ ] Feel energized and ready

If any items are concerning, execute the Conservative Option below instead.

---

**Warm-up (8-10 minutes):**

1. Walk or light jog in place: 2 minutes
2. Arm circles: 10 forward, 10 backward
3. Leg swings: 8 front-back each leg, 8 side-to-side each leg
4. Hip circles: 8 each direction, each leg
5. Bodyweight squats (partial range): 10 reps, slow and controlled
6. Glute bridges: 10 reps, focus on activation
7. Cat-cow stretches: 10 reps
8. World's greatest stretch: 3 each side

**Main Work (12-15 minutes):**

**A. Glute Bridges**
- Sets: 2
- Reps: 12-15
- Tempo: 2 seconds up, 1 second squeeze at top, 3 seconds down
- Rest: 60 seconds between sets
- Cues: Drive through heels, squeeze glutes at top, maintain neutral spine
- RPE: 4/10 (should feel activation, not strain)

**B. Dead Bugs**
- Sets: 2
- Reps: 8-10 each side (alternating)
- Tempo: Slow and controlled, 3 seconds per rep
- Rest: 60 seconds between sets
- Cues: Press lower back into floor, extend opposite arm/leg without arching back
- RPE: 4/10

**C. Clamshells**
- Sets: 2
- Reps: 15-20 each side
- Tempo: 1 second up, 1 second squeeze, 2 seconds down
- Rest: 45 seconds between sets
- Cues: Keep feet together, rotate from hip, feel glute medius (side of hip) working
- RPE: 4/10

**D. Single-Leg Balance**
- Sets: 2
- Duration: 30-45 seconds each leg
- Rest: 30 seconds between sets
- Cues: Soft knee, engage core, focus on stable hip, use wall for light support if needed
- RPE: 4/10

**Cool-down (5 minutes):**
1. Child's pose: 90 seconds
2. Supine figure-4 stretch: 60 seconds each side
3. Supine knee to chest: 30 seconds each leg
4. Deep breathing: 1-2 minutes

---

**Post-Session Assessment:**

Immediately after:
- How do you feel? (Should feel energized, not exhausted)
- Any joint discomfort? (Should be zero)
- Muscles feel worked but not strained? (Correct response)

Next morning:
- Any soreness? (Goal: ZERO soreness. If sore, reduce volume/intensity for Friday's session)

---

**CONSERVATIVE OPTION (If recovery is questionable):**

**Duration:** 15 minutes
**Focus:** Activation only, minimal volume

**Warm-up:** 5 minutes (as above, abbreviated)

**Main Work:**
- Glute bridges: 1 set × 10 reps
- Dead bugs: 1 set × 6 each side
- Clamshells: 1 set × 12 each side
- Single-leg balance: 1 set × 30 seconds each leg

**Cool-down:** 3-4 minutes

### Day 13 (Friday, December 6) - Race Day +13

**SECOND STRENGTH SESSION**

**Duration:** 20-30 minutes
**Intensity:** RPE 4-5 (same as Tuesday, or slightly progressed if you felt great)
**Load:** Bodyweight + optional light resistance band
**Goal:** Reinforce movement patterns, continue gentle progression

---

**SESSION 2: Pattern Reinforcement**

**Pre-Session Check:**
- [ ] No soreness from Tuesday's session (if soreness present, use Conservative Option)
- [ ] RHR at baseline
- [ ] Sleep adequate
- [ ] Energy good

---

**Warm-up (8-10 minutes):**

Same structure as Tuesday (or slightly abbreviated if time-limited):
- Light movement: 2 minutes
- Dynamic mobility: 5-6 minutes (arm circles, leg swings, hip circles, bodyweight squats, glute bridges)
- Specific prep: 2 minutes (lighter versions of main exercises)

**Main Work (12-15 minutes):**

**Option A - FULL (If Tuesday's session caused zero soreness):**

**A. Single-Leg Glute Bridge**
- Sets: 2
- Reps: 8-10 each leg
- Tempo: 2 seconds up, 1 second hold, 3 seconds down
- Rest: 60-75 seconds between sets
- Cues: Keep hips level, drive through heel, squeeze glute at top
- RPE: 5/10
- Regression: If too challenging, return to regular glute bridges (2×15)

**B. Bird Dogs**
- Sets: 2
- Reps: 8-10 each side (alternating)
- Tempo: Slow and controlled, 3-4 seconds per rep
- Rest: 60 seconds
- Cues: Maintain neutral spine, extend arm and leg without rotating torso
- RPE: 4-5/10

**C. Side-Lying Hip Abduction**
- Sets: 2
- Reps: 12-15 each side
- Tempo: 2 seconds up, 1 second hold, 2 seconds down
- Rest: 45 seconds
- Cues: Keep hips stacked, lift from hip (not by rolling back), feel glute medius
- RPE: 5/10
- Progression: Add light resistance band around thighs if too easy

**D. Calf Raises (Bodyweight)**
- Sets: 2
- Reps: 15-20 (slow and controlled)
- Tempo: 2 seconds up, 1 second hold, 3 seconds down
- Rest: 45 seconds
- Cues: Full range of motion, control eccentric, rise onto balls of feet
- RPE: 4-5/10
- Note: Do 1 set straight-leg (gastrocnemius), 1 set bent-knee/seated (soleus)

**E. Single-Leg Balance with Movement**
- Sets: 2
- Duration: 30-45 seconds each leg
- Movement: Small knee bends while balancing, or gentle reaches with opposite leg
- Rest: 30 seconds
- RPE: 5/10

---

## Troubleshooting Guide

**"I'm still sore on Day 7-8. When should I start strength?"**
- Extend complete rest through Day 9-10
- Assess again - if still sore, something else may be wrong (nutrition, sleep, underlying issue)
- Don't start strength until soreness fully resolves

**"Tuesday's session made me sore. What do I do for Friday?"**
- Execute Conservative Option (reduced volume)
- Extend Week 2 for an additional week before progressing to Week 3
- Document what made you sore (too many sets? specific exercise? overall volume?)

**"I feel great and Week 2 feels too easy. Can I progress faster?"**
- Resist the urge to rush
- Week 2 is designed to be easy - this is intentional
- Your body is still adapting to post-marathon state
- Patience now prevents setbacks in Weeks 3-6
- If Week 2 Full Options feel genuinely easy, that's perfect - you're ready for Week 3

**"My PTT is flaring up. What should I do?"**
- Stop all strength training immediately
- Return to PT protocol for PTT management
- Extend rest period until symptoms fully resolve
- When resuming, avoid: calf raises, single-leg work with load, explosive movements
- Focus on: glute work, core work, upper body

**"My sleep is terrible this week (newborn). Should I skip strength?"**
- If sleeping <5 hours consistently: Yes, skip strength training
- If sleeping 5-6.5 hours: Execute Conservative Options only
- If sleeping >6.5 hours: Execute Full Options
- Strength training requires adequate recovery - without it, it's counterproductive

**"Can I do strength on days other than Tuesday/Friday?"**
- Week 2: Yes, timing is flexible since volume is so light
- Week 3+: Prefer Tuesday (primary) and Friday (lighter) to coordinate with running schedule
- Avoid strength within 24 hours before quality running (Thursday) or long runs (Saturday)

**"I want to do more than 2 sessions per week. Can I?"**
- Week 2: No. Two sessions is plenty.
- Week 3-6: Still no. Quality over quantity.
- Week 7+: Potentially add a third very light session if recovery supports it, but 2 sessions done well is superior to 3 sessions done poorly

## Final Thoughts: The Power of Patience

These two weeks will feel almost laughably easy. Week 1, you'll do nothing. Week 2, you'll do very little. This is exactly correct.

The runners who rush back to training—including strength training—are the ones who:
- Experience extended recovery periods (weeks turn into months)
- Develop overuse injuries
- Feel perpetually fatigued for the next training cycle
- Sacrifice long-term progress for short-term activity

The runners who respect the recovery process:
- Emerge stronger than before
- Build sustainable training patterns
- Arrive at the next training cycle truly fresh
- Make consistent long-term progress

You've trained for months to run 26.2 miles. Invest two weeks to recover properly. The patience you practice now will compound across your entire running career.

Your post-marathon strength training journey starts with rest. Rest is training.
