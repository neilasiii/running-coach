## 2. Detailed Taper Plan: November 20 - November 23

The remaining days are about very light activity to keep the legs turning over, maintaining neuromuscular sharpness, and most importantly, rest and fueling.

---

**Today (Approx. November 20): Short Easy Run with Strides**

*   **Warmup:** 10 min E (10:00-11:10 pace)
*   **Main Set:** 4-6 x 20-second strides with ample walking recovery (e.g., 60-90 seconds walk). The strides should be **controlled and relaxed**, not all-out sprints. Focus on good form.
*   **Cooldown:** 5 min E (10:00-11:10 pace)
*   **Total Time:** Approximately 20-25 minutes
*   **Purpose:** To maintain leg speed and neuromuscular coordination without adding any fatigue. The short, fast bursts help to keep your fast-twitch muscle fibers activated and improve running economy, ensuring you don't feel sluggish on race day. Given the hard effort on Nov 15th, this session should feel *very* easy and controlled.
*   **Pacing Guidance:** Your easy pace should feel conversational, with a heart rate typically in the 130-145 bpm range. Strides are faster, but still controlled.
*   **Modification Options (if feeling fatigued):**
    *   **Conservative:** Skip the strides entirely and just do a 15-20 minute easy run. Focus purely on blood flow and recovery.
    *   **Moderate:** Reduce to 2-3 x 20-second strides.
    *   **Full (as prescribed):** Only if you feel completely recovered from the Nov 15th run.

## 3. Detailed Race Strategy: Central Florida Legends Skunk Ape Marathon

This is what all your training has been for. Trust your fitness, trust your taper, and execute your plan.

*   **Pre-Race Fueling (Gluten-Free, Dairy-Free):**
    *   **Evening Before:** A simple, easily digestible meal like plain rice with lean protein (e.g., chicken or fish) and cooked vegetables. Avoid high-fiber foods that could cause GI distress.
    *   **Race Morning (3-4 hours prior):** A familiar, light, carbohydrate-rich breakfast. This could be gluten-free toast with a thin layer of nut butter, or gluten-free oatmeal with a banana. Practice this exact meal during a long run simulation.
    *   **Hydration:** Sip water consistently throughout the morning.
    *   **Caffeine (if used):** Stick to your usual pre-run caffeine routine, if any.

*   **Warm-up:**
    *   **15-20 minutes before start:** A very gentle 5-10 minute easy jog (11:00-12:00/mile pace) followed by 2-3 light dynamic stretches (leg swings, arm circles). The goal is to activate muscles, not to fatigue them.

*   **Pacing Strategy (Target B Goal: Sub 4:30 / 10:00-10:18/mile):**
    *   **Start Conservatively (Miles 1-3):** Resist the urge to go out too fast. Allow your body to warm up naturally. Aim for the slower end of your target pace range (around 10:15-10:30/mile) or even slightly slower. Let others pass you.
    *   **Even Pacing (Miles 4-18):** Settle into your target pace of **10:00-10:18/mile**. Focus on consistent effort. Use your perceived effort (RPE) more than your watch if heart rate is elevated due to heat or excitement.
    *   **Assess and Adjust (Miles 18-22):** This is where the marathon truly begins. Listen to your body.
        *   If you feel strong and are hitting your target pace comfortably, maintain.
        *   If you're feeling fatigued, don't be afraid to slow down a few seconds per mile to maintain form and prevent bonking. The goal is to keep moving forward efficiently.
    *   **Finish Strong (Miles 22-26.2):** Focus on maintaining form and rhythm. If you have energy left, you can gradually increase effort in the final miles.

*   **Fueling During the Race:**
    *   **Gels:** Start taking your preferred gluten-free/dairy-free gels (Precision Fuel, Huma gels) around **mile 4-5**, and continue every **30-40 minutes**. Practice this exact schedule during your long runs. Have a plan for how many gels you'll take and when.
    *   **Electrolytes:** Supplement with Base salts or FastChews as practiced, especially given Central Florida's potential for warmth and humidity. This is crucial for preventing cramps and maintaining fluid balance.
    *   **Hydration:** Take water or electrolyte drinks at every aid station. Even if you don't feel thirsty, sip regularly to stay ahead of dehydration.

*   **Mental Strategy:**
    *   **Stay Present:** Focus on one mile at a time, or even one aid station at a time.
    *   **Body Scan:** Regularly check in with how you feel – form, breathing, effort.
    *   **Positive Self-Talk:** Remind yourself of your training and resilience.
    *   **Break it Down:** Think in smaller segments (e.g., "just get to the next aid station," "just run for 10 more minutes").

*   **Injury Prevention:**
    *   **Listen to your Posterior Tibialis:** At any sign of discomfort, adjust your form, ease your pace, or even walk briefly if necessary. Finishing healthy is the absolute priority. Don't push through sharp pain.
    *   **Maintain Form:** As you fatigue, your form can break down. Actively think about standing tall, quick cadence, and relaxed shoulders.

## 4. Post-Race Recovery (Brief Overview)

Immediately after the marathon, your body will need significant recovery.

*   **First 24-48 hours:** Prioritize rest, easy walking, gentle stretching, and consistent refueling with nutrient-dense foods.
*   **Next 2-3 weeks:** Focus on active recovery with very easy, short runs (30-45 min E pace) only when you feel completely ready, interspersed with cross-training and mobility. This is a crucial period to avoid injury and rebuild your energy stores before beginning any structured training for the Gasparilla Half Marathon in February.
